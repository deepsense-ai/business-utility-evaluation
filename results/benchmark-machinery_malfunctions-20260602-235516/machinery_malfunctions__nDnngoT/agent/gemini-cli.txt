### Summary of Findings & Methodology

1. **Attribution Modeling**: We mapped every single production batch to the unique maintenance event that immediately preceded it on that machine. A batch was linked to a maintenance work order only if it started after that maintenance finished, and before the next maintenance event on that machine started.
2. **Isolating Maintenance-Caused Rejections (Hidden Faults)**:
   - A maintenance-caused "hidden fault" persists and affects all subsequent batches of the affected product on that machine until the next maintenance occurs.
   - We analyzed intervals where a product was rejected and checked for other batches of the same product in that interval.
   - We discovered that in **partially rejected intervals** (where some batches of the product were accepted and others were rejected), rejections are strongly explained by non-maintenance factors (specifically, a strong correlation with **low and medium operator experience**).
   - In contrast, in **pure failure intervals** (where 100% of the batches of a product produced in that interval were rejected), rejections were independent of operator experience and daily factory conditions. This confirmed these intervals as the genuine instances where a hidden fault was introduced by a technician's faulty maintenance.
3. **Technician Confirmation**:
   - Applying the criteria to these pure failure events, we identified exactly **10 confirmed technicians** who were linked to $\ge 3$ distinct rejected batches distributed across $\ge 2$ different work orders.
   - Technicians who did not meet both of these thresholds (e.g. `TECH_010`, who had 5 rejected batches but all on a single work order, or `TECH_020`, who only had 2 rejected batches) were excluded.
4. **Final Mapping**:
   - We compiled the list of products affected by each confirmed technician based on the pure failure intervals, sorted the keys and values, and wrote the final result to `/app/answer.json`.
